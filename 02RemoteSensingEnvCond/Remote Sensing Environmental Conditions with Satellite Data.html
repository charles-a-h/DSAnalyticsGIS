Remote Sensing Environmental Conditions with Satellite Data

Classifying Land Use, Vegetative Index, & additional factors to Obtain Land Surface Temperature for Analyzing Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect

Charles A. Hulebak

June 3, 2021

Introduction and Overview

Urban heat islands (UHI) are the result of developing natural habitats with dense concentrations of buildings, structures, pavement, and other surfaces that absorb and retain heat.  Energy from this heat is stored within the materials and released during diurnal temperature cycles, increasing the overall mean temperature and the microclimate within these locations. 

The increased UHI creates an energy demand and associated cost for interior climate control, which is also related to higher air pollution (depending on the energy source), and additionally negatively impacts health conditions including heat related illnesses and mortality rates.

There are solutions to reduce the overall UHI, some examples include increased tree canopies and vegetation to cover these materials and reflect the light, capital improvement investments for paved surfaces that have more porous materials, integrating roofing solutions for buildings and structures, including reflective materials with low energy absorption rates and green roofs. This project will not focus on implementing these solutions, rather the scientific analysis of the UHI within this area. Process steps can be performed to determine locations, historical changes, as well as the measurement of implemented solutions for city officials and the public to measure.

Process

The initial step to quantify the UHI includes measurement of the Land Surface Temperature (LST) within an area of interest. Many aeras (areas) of research include the measurement of the LST, which span from environmental, urban land use and cover, agricultural, hydrological, and socio-economic processes. The process of remote sensing images provides the ability to calculate the LST, which are obtained from Landsat 8 satellite imagery. LST is calculated from a combination of factors, which include Normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI), brightness temperature (BT), and land surface emissivity (LSE). The process steps to develop the LST are included in the context of this project.

The overall UHI is analyzed for the county of Los Angeles, California. The project will focus on obtaining the land surface temperature variation for the county, while understanding that this data can be interpreted by using the land use classifications, as well as identifying various places within the county and the ability to compare and contrast the temperature variations.

This project will focus on the process steps to obtain the Land Surface Temperature within a region.  The most effective method to use this type of analysis is for time-series, and using a similar methodology and various points in time to determine accuracy.  This information can also be compared following the opportunity to introduce solutions into the built environment.

The normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) is an assessment method used to observe whether an area contains live green vegetation. Calculating the NDVI is a necessary process step to obtain the land surface temperature (LST). NDVI analysis requires use of bands 2, 3, 4, and 5.

Estimating the Land Surface Temperature (LST)

The land surface temperature can be considered to be how hot or cold the temperature of the earth would be to touch. Understanding the LST is an important process to understanding complex systems that may relate to land health, extreme events such as natural disasters, and others such as the urban heat island effect.

Process Overview of creating the LST

The measurement of the LST is a multistep process that includes three primary components, which include:

  • Converting the Landsat data to Top of Atmosphere (TOA) spectral radiance
  • Conversion to Top of Atmosphere Brightness Temperature (to obtain ground surface temperature)
  • Calculating emissivity
  • Using the NDVI and previous calculations to measure the LST

Conversion to TOA Radiance Process

Using the following formula, the Landsat Level-1 data can be converted to TOA spectral radiance using the radiance rescaling factors in the MTL metadata file included with the imagery data. The formula for TOA spectral radiance is:

L = ML* Qcal + AL

where:

  • L = TOA spectral radiance (Watts/( m2 * srad * m))
  • ML =Band-specific multiplicative rescaling factor from the metadata (RADIANCE_MULT_BAND_x, where x is the band number)
  • AL=Band-specific additive rescaling factor from the metadata (RADIANCE_ADD_BAND_x, where x is the band number)
  • Qcal =  Quantized and calibrated standard product pixel values (DN)

The TOA temperature is not considered to be a good estimate of the surface temperature without the inclusion of measuring the atmospheric correction. The atmospheric interaction and use of a radiative transfer model is used to remove atmospheric effects. There are several methods to accounting for this correction, which include:

  1. Thermal Infrared Sensor (TIRS) Bias Correction, which subtracts the atmospheric effect from the TOA spectral radiance using referenced values (less accurate).[1]
  2. Performing the Land Surface Temperature calculation by obtaining surface radiance then converting into surface kinetic temperatures by inverting a simplified Plancks law to the specific spectral window by spectral integration (more accurate).[2]

The spectral distribution of the radiation from a blackbody is described by Plancks law:

B(T) =C1 / 5 [exp(C2 / T)1]

A radiative transfer model can be used to estimate the unknowns terms provided the corresponding vertical atmospheric composition is known, taking into account the atmospheric interaction and using a radiative transfer model to remove the atmospheric effects.

L,sen(T) = s,B(T) + Lu,,atmo + (1s,)Ld,,atmo

where:

  • L,sen= the spectral at-sensor radiance (top of the atmosphere) (Wm2sr1m1)
  • B= the radiance of a supposed blackbody surface target at a kinetic temperature Tin (K)
  • = the atmospheric transmittance (unitless) at the wavelength (m)
  • Lu,,atmo= the upwelling atmospheric radiance in the wavelength window (Wm2sr1m1)
  • Ld,,atmo= the downwelling atmospheric radiance in the wavelength window (Wm2sr1m1)
  • s,= the surface spectral emissivity (unitless)

The conversion of the at-sensor radiance into surface radiance that is not susceptible to atmospheric effects can be derived using spectral atmospheric parameters (, Lu and Ld) and the surface emissivity(s). Plancks law is inverted to specific sensor spectral integration using the following formula:

LST = K2 / ln(1+K1 / B(T))

where:

  • LST= equivalent to T, the land surface temperature (K)
  • B(T)= the surface radiance (Wm2sr1m1)
  • K1= the pre-launch calibration constant 1 (Wm2sr1m1)
  • K2= the pre-launch calibration constant 2 (K)
  • K1 and K2 are provided by the USGS for L5 and L7 (24), and for L8 (25)

Atmospheric profiles are generated by the United States National Centers for Environmental Prediction (NCEP), and include satellite and surface data to predict a global atmosphere. The atmospheric profiles are sampled on a 1x1 degree grid and generated every six hours. Obtaining the values from NASAs atmospheric correction parameter calculator provides the ability to measure the atmospheric profile using one of two methods, which include the closest integer or interpolation for the latitude/longitude parameters (most accurate).

Top of Atmosphere (TOA) Spectral Radiance Calculation

The Top of Atmosphere (TOA) can be calculated using the following formula:

L = ML* Qcal + AL - Oi

where:

  • L = TOA spectral radiance (Watts/(m2 * srad * m))
  • ML =Band-specific multiplicative rescaling factor from the metadata (RADIANCE_MULT_BAND_x, where x is the band number)
  • AL=Band-specific additive rescaling factor from the metadata (RADIANCE_ADD_BAND_x, where x is the band number)
  • Qcal =  Quantized and calibrated standard product pixel values (DN)
  • Oi = Bias Correction for band 10 (-0.29 0.12)

* Emissivity, , at a given wavelength (units, m) and temperature T (units, K), is the ratio of the radiance R (T) emitted by a body of temperature T and the radiance B (T) (Plancks constant) emitted by a black body at the same temperature T. The formula was derived from the proposed theoretical model by Valor and Caselles that relates to the emissivity to the NDVI of a given surface by: [4]

= VPV + s(1PV) + 4(d)PV (1PV)

where:

  • v = emissivity of vegetation
  • s = emissivity of soil
  • d = density of states, which accounts for the cavity effect, which depends on the surface geometry
  • v and s represent the emissivity of vegetation and soil, d is a term that accounts for the cavity effect, which depends on the surface geometry.

Analyzing the Heat Island Effect

There are various methods to analyze the data, now that we understand the land use classification, as well as the land surface temperature. When looking at this data comparatively in image 20, it is evident that there is not a completely accurate supervised classification for the land use. This accuracy can be increased by using unsupervised learning methods, algorithms and scripts that can distinguish between land use types. For the intentions of this example, we will compare the total area of land use (in acres), to the mean (average) temperature within the bounds. For example, the bare soil temperature range has a minimum of 38.6 C (70.6 F), and maximum of 47.5 C (79.5 F), with a mean temperature of 43.1C (75.1 F). The total acreage of bare soil will be multiplied by the mean temperature to obtain an estimate of the total heat on the land surface for the classification type. In this case, there are 650105 acres (area) of bare soil within the county, with a mean temperature of 43.1C, resulting in 27,987,020.25 (acres/temp). This value can be further calculated to obtain heat loss (or gain) over time for given materials.

The heat loss (or gain) can be calculated with the formula shown above, however we have only analyzed one period of time and do not have the material properties of the land classifications to establish an R-Value. However, this analysis will provide the ability to understand the relationships between the classes and the land surface temperature. This process can be further elaborated by performing the heat loss calculation to understand variation over time, and the impact that various classifications will make for the overall heat index.

Using the previous method described, we can conclude from this analysis that bare soil in the county is the largest classification contributor toward land surface temperature, followed by urban areas, forests, agriculture and water. 

What is important to understand are the ratios between these factors.  The ratio of 1 unit (acre in this case) of the classification type divided by the temperature provides the ability elaborate on our understanding of land use classification and the heat island effect. The ratio of these values indicates that the largest to smallest heat index includes bare soil, urban, agriculture, forest, and water. The graph shown above may be misleading, however for this county there are more forests than agriculture areas within this region.

Results and Conclusion

The results from this project were positive, and produced with a high level of accuracy for this large area.  However, this data was only a snapshot in time within an area and would require a multitude of time-series information to develop a better understanding of the environment. 

In addition, the project classifies five separate types of conditions, however these classes can be classified using a higher distribution, as well understanding the material properties (R Values) that may contribute to the urban heat island effect.

Furthermore, the supervised classification of these conditions was limited to taking random samples of similar conditions (i.e. rooftops of buildings for urban areas, bare soil in undeveloped areas, etc.). This classification method required an extensive amount of A/B testing on multiple layers to find a model that had a reasonable level of accuracy. This model is not reflective of the actual conditions and there is room to improve these classifications through the process of using either supervised or unsupervised classification processes. There are many other unsupervised learning models that are progressing within machine learning that may provide more accurate classifications from the image.

Lastly, the LST and classification models can be used in comparison with one another to develop a more comprehensive understanding of median temperature ranges and variation between the classes. Some of the strategies were limited within ArcMap to perform these tasks and are more suitable for exploration within the ArcGIS Pro or other geographical information systems suites.

In conclusion, this analysis did indicate that urban developments do result in a higher temperature than those classified with agriculture, vegetation, and water. This temperature increase does create an increased microclimate within the area related to the urban heat island effect. To obtain a more thorough understanding of the impact from UHI, it is necessary to examine these conditions over a longer period of time, as well as utilizing best industry practices to measure the effects within constraints. This analysis provides the ability to establish a better understanding of the underlying processes that are associated with image classification, processes required to classify land surface temperatures, opportunities for future scientific analysis, as well as how this type of analysis can influence improvements to better control Urban Heat Islands and microclimates.
